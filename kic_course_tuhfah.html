Biography

The Author

Sulaymān al-Jamzūrī

سليمان الجمزوري

A renowned Qur’an reciter from Egypt, best known for authoring this very poem, Tuhfat al-Atfal.

He was known as al-Afandī.

Full Name
Sulaymān ibn Ḥusayn ibn Muḥammad al-Jamzūrī
Origin (Nisba)
Jamzūr, a village in the district of Ṭanṭā, Egypt.
Birth
Born in Rabīʿ al-Awwal in the mid-1100s AH (approx. 1160s AH).
Notable Timeline
The exact year of his death is unknown. However, he was alive in 1208 AH, the year he completed his book “al-Fatḥ al-Raḥmānī.”
Verses 1-5

Introduction

The poem begins with the author expressing his hope for Allah's mercy.

He dedicates this poem, "The Children's Bequest", to students who wish to master the rules of Nun, Tanween, and Madd.

He acknowledges his teacher, Sheikh al-Meehi, and prays that this work brings benefit and reward.

Verse 1
يَقُولُ رَاجِي رَحْمَةِ الْغَفُورِ ... دَوْمًا سُلَيْمَانُ هُوَ الْجَمْزُورِي
Says he who steadily longs for the mercy of the All-Forgiving Benefactor, Sulayman, who is from Jamzur.
Verse 2
الْحَمْدُ لِلَّهِ مُصَلِّيًا عَلَى ... مُحَمَّدٍ وَآلِهِ وَمَنْ تَلاَ
Praise be to Allah for allowing me to send prayers and reverence, Upon Muhammad, his kin and those who followed him in deference.
Verse 3
وَبَعْدُ هَذَا النَّظْمُ لِلْمُرِيدِ ... فِي النُّونِ وَالتَّنْوِينِ وَالْمُدُودِ
Following that, this poem is for those who yearn to know, the Nun Sakin, Tanween and Madd rules as they go.
Verse 4
سَمَّيْتُهُ بِتُحْفَةِ الأَطْفَالِ ... عَنْ شَيْخِنَا الْمِيهِيِّ ذِي الْكَمَالِ
I have resolved to title it "the Children’s Bequest," and relate from my shaykh al-Meehi these precepts most perfect.
Verse 5
أَرْجُو بِهِ أَنْ يَنْفَعَ الطُّلاَّبَا ... وَالأَجْرَ وَالْقَبُولَ وَالثَّوَابَا
I cherish that this proves of worth to its students, and brings with it reward, reception and recompense.
Verses 6-7

Nun Sakin: Izhar

Izhar means clarity. This is the first rule of Nun Sakin and Tanween.

If the Nun is followed by any of the 6 Throat Letters, it must be pronounced clearly without an extra Ghunnah.

  • Hamza, Ha (هـ)
  • 'Ain, Ha (ح)
  • Ghain, Kha
Verse 6
لِلنُّونِ إِنْ تَسْكُنْ وَلِلتَّنْوِينِ ... أَرْبَعُ أَحْكَامٍ فَخُذْ تَبْيِينِ
For the nun without vowels and the tanween diction, exist four basic rules, so take my depiction.
Verse 7
فَالأَوَّلُ الإِظْهَارُ قَبْلَ أَحْرُفِ ... لِلْحَلْقِ سِتٌّ رُتِّبَتْ فَلْتَعْرِفِ
The first of these, the rule of Izhar, is before the letters hence, arising from the throat, six in number, in this known sequence:
Verse 8
هَمْزٌ فَهَاءٌ ثُمَّ عَيْنٌ حَاءُ ... مُهْمَلَتَانِ ثُمَّ غَيْنٌ خَاءُ
Hamza and Ha, then 'Ain and Ha, without the marks, then Ghain and Kha.
Verses 9-12

Nun Sakin: Idgham

Idgham means merging. It occurs with the letters of Yarmaloon (ي ر م ل و ن).

Two Types:

  • With Ghunnah: Yanmu (ي ن م و)
  • Without Ghunnah: Lam, Ra

Exception: If Nun and the letter are in the same word (e.g., Dunya), do not merge (Izhar Mutlaq).

Verse 9
وَالثَّانِ إِدْغَامٌ بِسِتَّةٍ أَتَتْ ... فِي يَرْمَلُونَ عِنْدَهُمْ قَدْ ثَبَتَتْ
The second is Idgham, with six it takes effect, in yarmaloon, a mnemonic which they accept.
Verse 10
لَكِنَّهَا قِسْمَانِ قِسْمٌ يُدْغَمَا ... فِيهِ بِغُنَّةٍ بِيَنْمُو عُلِمَا
But this is of two types, with the first being verbalized, with the trait of ghunna, and by yanmu recognized.
Verse 11
إِلاَّ إِذَا كَانَا بِكِلْمَةٍ فَلاَ ... تُدْغِمْ كَدُنْيَا ثُمَّ صِنْوَانٍ تَلاَ
Except if this occurs within one word, then there is none, no idgham in articulation, as in dunya and sinwan.
Verse 12
وَالثَّانِ إِدْغَامٌ بِغَيْرِ غُنَّهْ ... فِي اللاَّمِ وَالرَّا ثُمَّ كَرِّرَنَّهْ
The second type is Idgham without the ghunna trait, in lam and raa, but the latter you must reverberate.
Verse 13

Nun Sakin: Iqlab

Iqlab means conversion.

When Nun Sakin or Tanween is followed by the letter Baa (ب), the Nun turns into a hidden Meem with Ghunnah.

Example: Min Ba'di becomes Mimba'di.
Verse 13
وَالثَّالِثُ الإِقْلاَبُ عِنْدَ الْبَاءِ ... مِيمًا بِغُنَّةٍ مَعَ الإِخْفَاءِ
The third rule is Iqlab which occurs with the letter baa, which is converted to meem and pronounced with Ikhfaa.
Verses 14-16

Nun Sakin: Ikhfa

Ikhfa means hiding. It applies to the remaining 15 letters.

The sound is held between Izhar and Idgham, with a Ghunnah.

The letters are collected in the opening letter of each word in verse 16 (Sif Dha Thana...).

Verse 14
وَالرَّابِعُ الإِخْفَاءُ عِنْدَ الْفَاضِلِ ... مِنَ الْحُرُوفِ وَاجِبٌ لِلْفَاضِلِ
The fourth is Ikhfa for the respected student, with specified letters mandatory for the student.
Verse 15
فِي خَمْسَةٍ مِنْ بَعْدِ عَشْرٍ رَمْزُهَا ... فِي كِلْمِ هَذَا البَيْتِ قَد ضَّمَّنْتُهَا
In fifteen letters it takes effect, within this prose that I erect:
Verse 16
صِفْ ذَا ثَنَا كَمْ جَادَ شَخْصٌ قَدْ سَمَا ... دُمْ طَيِّبًا زِدْ فِي تُقًى ضَعْ ظَالِمَا
Relate of the praiseworthy one, how excellent is he who achieves status robust; Be ever perpetual in virtue, cultivate piety, and fend off the one who is unjust.
Verse 17

Meem & Nun Mushaddad

A simple but critical rule: Whenever Nun or Meem has a Shaddah (double accent), you must hold the Ghunnah (nasal sound) for 2 counts.

They are called "Letters of Ghunnah".

Verse 17
وَغُنَّ مِيمًا ثُمَّ نُونًا شُدِّدَا ... وَسَمِّ كُلاًّ حَرْفَ غُنَّةٍ بَدَا
And articulate ghunnah of the nun and meem that carries the double accent, and refer to both as letters of ghunna, as is obvious and apparent.
Verses 18-22

Meem Sakin

When Meem has no vowel, there are 3 rules:

  1. Ikhfa Shafawi: Before Baa.
  2. Idgham: Before another Meem.
  3. Izhar Shafawi: Before all other letters.

*Warning: Be careful to be clear before Waw and Fa.

Verse 18
وَالْمِيمُ إِنْ تَسْكُنْ تَجِي قَبْلَ الْهِجَا ... لاَ أَلِفٍ لَيِّنَةٍ لِذِي الْحِجَا
When stopping on Meem before the letters of the alphabet, but not before the Alif Layyinah, for he who is intelligent...
Verse 19
أَحْكَامُهَا ثَلاَثَةٌ لِمَنْ ضَبَطْ ... إِخْفَاءٌ ادْغَامٌ وَإِظْهَارٌ فَقَطْ
Are three rules for he who would save them to memory, and they are the rules of Ikhfa, Idgham and Izhar only.
Verse 20
فَالأَوَّلُ الإِخْفَاءُ عِنْدَ الْبَاءِ ... وَسَمِّهِ الشَّفْوِيَّ لِلْقُرَّاءِ
The first is Ikhfa with the baa letter, termed Labial Ikhfa by the recitors.
Verse 21
وَالثَّانِ إِدْغَامٌ بِمِثْلِهَا أَتَى ... وَسَمِّ إِدْغَامًا صَغِيرًا يَا فَتَى
The second is Idgham when its likeness (meem) appears, and name it the Lesser Idgham my dears.
Verse 22
وَالثَّالِثُ الإِظْهَارُ فِي الْبَقِيَّهْ ... مِنْ أَحْرُفٍ وَسَمِّهَا شَفْوِيَّهْ
The third is Izhar which occurs with the balance of the letters, and termed Labial Izhar in our parlance.
Verse 23 (Warning)
وَاحْذَرْ لَدَى وَاوٍ وَفَا أَنْ تَخْتَفِي ... لِقُرْبِهَا وَالاتِّحَادِ فَاعْرِفِ
And be wary of making Ikhfa with waw and faa when you read, due to the closeness and unity of its makhraj, so take heed.
Verses 24-28

Lam of "Al" (The)

Lam Qamariya (Lunar): Pronounced clearly. Occurs before 14 letters (e.g., Al-Qamar).

Lam Shamsiyya (Solar): Merged (Silent). Occurs before 14 letters (e.g., Ash-Shams).

Verse 24
لِلاَمِ أَلْ حَالَانِ قَبْلَ الأَحْرُفِ ... أُولاَهُمَا إِظْهَارُهَا فَلْتَعْرِفِ
The lam of the definite article exists in two states before the letters, the first is its manifest articulation...
Verse 25
قَبْلَ ارْبَعٍ مَعْ عَشْرَةٍ خُذْ عِلْمَهُ ... مِنِ ابْغِ حَجَّكَ وَخَفْ عَقِيمَهُ
Occuring before fourteen letters... from this mnemonic: "Seek your goal and futile pursuits repel."
Verse 26
ثَانِيهِمَا إِدْغَامُهَا فِي أَرْبَعِ ... وَعَشْرَةٍ أَيْضًا وَرَمْزُهَا فَعِ
The second is its assimilation, which in fourteen does exist, and likewise, facilitate your recollection of them from this:
Verse 27
طِبْ ثُمَّ صِلْ رُحْمًا تَفُزْ ضِفْ ذَا نِعَمْ ... دَعْ سُوءَ ظَنٍّ زُرْ شَرِيفًا لِلْكَرَمْ
Be meritorious, maintain relations for success... (Mnemonic for Solar Letters).
Verse 28
وَاللاَّمَ الأُولَى سَمِّهَا قَمْرِيَّهْ ... وَاللاَّمَ الأُخْرَى سَمِّهَا شَمْسِيَّهْ
Name the first Lam Qamariya, and the latter Lam Shamsiyya.
Verses 29-31

The Verbal Lam

Unlike the Lam of "Al", the Lam inside a verb is always clear (Izhar).

Example: Qul (Say), Iltaqa (Met).

Verse 29
وَأَظْهِرَنَّ لاَمَ فِعْلٍ مُطْلَقَا ... فِي نَحْوِ قُلْ نَعَمْ وَقُلْنَا وَالْتَقَى
And pronounce with manifestation the verbal lam consistently, in words like Qul Naam, Qulna, Waltaqaa and their variety.
Verses 32-36

Letter Relationships

Letters are related by their output (Makhraj) and attributes (Sifat).

  • Mithlayn: Identical (e.g. Baa & Baa).
  • Mutaqaribayn: Close (e.g. Qaf & Kaf).
  • Mutajanisayn: Same Makhraj, Diff Sifat (e.g. Ta & Da).
Verse 30
إِنْ فِي الصِّفَاتِ وَالْمَخَارِجِ اتَّفَقْ ... حَرْفَانِ فَالْمِثْلاَنِ فِيهِمَا أَحَقْ
If two letters agree in attribute and point of articulation, then it is more deserving to use the Mithlayn designation.
Verse 31
وَإِنْ يَكُونَا مَخْرَجًا تَقَارَبَا ... وَفِي الصِّفَاتِ اخْتَلَفَا يُلَقَّبَا
But if the two in their points of articulation be akin, yet in their attributes varying then use the heading...
Verse 32
مُتَقَارِبَيْنِ أَوْ يَكُونَا اتَّفَقَا ... فِي مَخْرَجٍ دُونَ الصِّفَاتِ حُقِّقَا
Mutaqaribayn; And if they so happen to concur in articulation but not attribute then they deserve...
Verse 33
بِالْمُتَجَانِسَيْنِ ثُمَّ إِنْ سَكَن ... أَوَّلُ كُلٍّ فَالصَّغِيرَ سَمِّيَن
Mutajanisayn; And if its status be unvowelled of the first letter in each case, the Lesser is the title.
Verse 34
أَوْ حُرِّكَ الْحَرْفَانِ فِي كُلٍّ فَقُلْ ... كُلٌّ كَبِيرٌ وَافْهَمَنْهُ بِالْمُثُلْ
And if both the letters are vowelled in all the situations, then term it the Greater, and learn this with illustrations.
Verses 37-43

Madd (Elongation)

Natural (Asli): The innate length (2 counts) without an external cause.

Derived (Far'i): Extended length caused by Hamza or Sukoon.

The Madd letters are Alif, Waw, and Ya.

Verse 35
وَالْمَدُّ أَصْلِيٌّ وَ فَرْعِيٌّ لَهُ ... وَسَمِّ أَوَّلاً طَبِيعِيًّا وَهُوَ
Mad exists in two types, they being Natural and Derived, so name the initial one Natural...
Verse 36
مَا لاَ تَوَقُّفٌ لَهُ عَلَى سَبَبْ ... وَلاَ بِدُونِهِ الْحُرُوفُ تُجْتَلَبْ
Of that madd which on a cause does not rely, and neither can its letters without it be realized.
Verse 37
بَلْ أَيُّ حَرْفٍ غَيْرُ هَمْزٍ أَوْ سُكُونْ ... جَا بَعْدَ مَدٍّ فَالطَّبِيعِيَّ يَكُونْ
But if any letter, barring hamza, or sukoon at hand follows the madd, then arises the Natural brand.
Verse 38
وَالآخَرُ الْفَرْعِيُّ مَوْقُوفٌ عَلَى ... سَبَبْ كَهَمْزٍ أَوْ سُكُونٍ مُسْجَلاَ
The second type is the Derived madd which rests upon a cause, namely, always on the hamza letter or sukoon.
Verse 39
حُرُوفُهُ ثَلاَثَةٌ فَعِيهَا ... مِنْ لَفْظِ وَايٍ وَهْيَ فِي نُوحِيهَا
The madd has three letters, so know them as explained, in the mnemonic wai and in the word nuuheehaa contained.
Verse 40
وَالْكَسْرُ قَبْلَ الْيَا وَقَبْلَ الْوَاوِ ضَمْ ... شَرْطٌ وَفَتْحٌ قَبْلَ أَلْفٍ يُلْتَزَمْ
And kasra before the letter ya, and waw with dhamma preceding, is surely a condition, whereas the fatha before the alif is binding.
Verse 41
وَاللِّينُ مِنْهَا الْيَا وَوَاوٌ سُكِّنَا ... إِنِ انْفِتَاحٌ قَبْلَ كُلٍّ أُعْلِنَا
And from these are the Layyin letters: waw and ya sakin, when preceded by the vowel fatha, as is plainly lucent.
Verses 44-47

Madd Rules

Wajib (Muttasil): Hamza in same word. (4-5 counts).

Ja'iz (Munfasil): Hamza in next word. (Allowed to shorten or lengthen).

Lazim: Permanent Sukoon follows.

Verse 42
لِلْمَدِّ أَحْكَامٌ ثَلاَثَةٌ تَدُومْ ... وَهْيَ الْوُجُوبُ وَالْجَوَازُ وَاللُّزُومْ
The derived madd, its rules are always three; and they are Wajib, Ja’iz and Lazim only.
Verse 43
فَوَاجِبٌ إِنْ جَاءَ هَمْزٌ بَعْدَ مَدْ ... فِي كِلْمَةٍ وَذَا بِمُتَّصِلْ يُعَدْ
And Wajib is when hamza, after a letter of madd does follow, in one single word, and this is termed Madd Muttasil also.
Verse 44
وَجَائِزٌ مَدٌّ وَقَصْرٌ إِنْ فُصِلْ ... كُلٌّ بِكِلْمَةٍ وَهَذَا الْمُنْفَصِلْ
And Jaiz is when prolongation and shortening are acceptable, when they are in separate words... Madd Munfasil.
Verse 45
وَمِثْلُ ذَا إِنْ عَرَضَ السُّكُونُ ... وَقْفًا كَتَعْلَمُونَ نَسْتَعِينُ
And likewise is the case where the sukoon is created, from stopping, in the words ta‘lamoon and nasta‘een illustrated.
Verse 46
أَوْ قُدِّمَ الْهَمْزُ عَلَى الْمَدِّ وَذَا ... بَدَلْ كَآمَنُوا وَإِيمَانًا خُذَا
But in those cases the hamza precedes the madd letter, arises the Madd Badal, in aamanu and imaanaa illustrated.
Verse 47
وَلاَزِمٌ إِنِ السُّكُونُ أُصِّلاَ ... وَصْلاً وَوَقْفًا بَعْدَ مَدٍّ طُوِّلاَ
And Lazim occurs when real is the stop, after the madd, in flow and stop, and in length there can be no drop.
Verses 48-53

Madd Lazim

Occurs when Sukoon is permanent. It is split into Word (Kalimi) and Letter (Harfi).

Each is further split into Heavy (Muthaqqal - with Shaddah) or Light (Mukhaffaf - without Shaddah).

Verse 48
أَقْسَامُ لاَزِمٍ لَدَيْهِمْ أَرْبَعَهْ ... وَتِلْكَ كِلْمِيٌّ وَحَرْفِيٌّ مَعَهْ
The types of Lazim among them are four, and they are either the Kalimi or Harfi sort.
Verse 49
كِلاَهُمَا مُخَفَّفٌ مُثَقَّلُ ... فَهَذِهِ أَرْبَعَةٌ تُفَصَّلُ
And each of these into the Light or Heavy division, a total of four, with this subsequent exposition.
Verse 50
فَإِنْ بِكِلْمَةٍ سُكُونٌ اجْتَمَعْ ... مَعْ حَرْفِ مَدٍّ فَهْوَ كِلْمِيٌّ وَقَعْ
And if a word embraces sukoon in combination, with a madd, it carries the Kalimi designation.
Verse 51
أَوْ فِي ثُلاَثِيِّ الْحُرُوفِ وُجِدَا ... وَالْمَدُّ وَسْطُهُ فَحَرْفِيٌّ بَدَا
And if in three disjointed letters both arise, with madd at the center, then the Harfi term applies.
Verse 52
كِلاَهُمَا مُثَقَّلٌ إِنْ أُدْغِمَا ... مُخَفَّفٌ كُلٌّ إِذَا لَمْ يُدْغَمَا
Both of these are muthaqqal in the case of idgham, and conversely mukhaffaf when there is no idgham.
Verses 54-56

Harfi Letters

These rules apply to the "Disjointed Letters" opening some Surahs.

Lazim (6 Counts): Kam 'Asal Naqas (8 letters).

Natural (2 Counts): Hayyun Tahir (5 letters).

No Madd: Alif.

Verse 53
وَاللَّازِمُ الْحَرْفِيُّ أَوَّلَ السُّوَرْ ... وُجُودُهُ وَفِي ثَمَانٍ انْحَصَرْ
The Lazim Harfi Madd in the start of the chapters is found, and encompassed in eight distinct letters...
Verse 54
يَجْمَعُهَا حُرُوفُ كَمْ عَسَلْ نَقَصْ ... وَعَيْنُ ذُو وَجْهَيْنِ وَالطُّولُ أَخَصْ
Contained within the "Kam ‘Asal Naqas" expression; and while ‘ayn has two ways, preferred is prolongation.
Verse 55
وَمَا سِوَى الْحَرْفِ الثُّلاَثِي لاَ أَلِفْ ... فَمَدُّهُ مَدًّا طَبِيعِيًّا أُلِفْ
And those apart from the trilateral, alif excluding, for their madd, the natural type is more deserving.
Verse 56
وَذَاكَ أَيْضًا فِي فَوَاتِحِ السُّوَرْ ... فِي لَفْظِ حَيٍّ طَاهِرٍ قَدِ انْحَصَرْ
And these are also found in the openings of the chapters, and in the expression Hayyun Tahirun captured.
Verse 57
وَيَجْمَعُ الْفَوَاتِحَ الأَرْبَعْ عَشَرْ ... صِلْهُ سُحَيْرًا مَنْ قَطَعْكَ ذَا اشْتَهَرْ
And the fourteen of the chapter openings are arrayed, in what they say: Restore the bonds without delay, of the kin that cut the way.
Conclusion

Khatimah (The End)

The poem concludes with praise to Allah and prayers upon the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

"Its verses are fragrant for those who possess ingenuity..."

Verse 58
وَتَمَّ ذَا النَّظْمُ بِحَمْدِ اللَّهِ ... عَلَى تَمَامِهِ بِلاَ تَنَاهِي
This ode comes now to its conclusion, with profuse thanks to Allah for its completion.
Verse 59
أَبْيَاتُهُ نَدٌّ بَدَا لِذِي النُّهَى ... تَارِيخُهَا بُشْرَى لِمَنْ يُتْقِنُهَا
Its verses are fragrant for those who possess ingenuity, and its date... salutation for those who commit it to memory.
Verse 60
ثُمَّ الصَّلاَةُ وَالسَّلاَمُ أَبَدَا ... عَلَى خِتَامِ الأَنْبِيَاءِ أَحْمَدَا
And eternal may His salutations be, upon Ahmad, the Seal of the Prophecy.
Verse 61
وَالآلِ وَالصَّحْبِ وَكُلِّ تَابِعِ ... وَكُلِّ قَارِئٍ وَكُلِّ سَامِعِ
As well as on his noble family and Companions, and every follower, recitor and those who listen.
